PERSPECTIVES ON THE CASE

The trial taking place on November 20th is regaurding, the accused, Meaursault of 1st degree murder. I talked to many citizens involved in the case to find out why it is so controversial. I talked to Meursault’s closest acquaintances and his Head Defense Attorney, as well as the victim’s friend and Head Prosecution Attorney.

First I addressed to Monsieur Masson who is one of Meursault’s acquaintances. He owns the beach house where the murder was committed, and is good friends with Monsieur Raymond, which is how he knows Meursault. He believes that Meursault on first impression was “a little arrogant maybe or a little self-centered,” and went on to describe him as “not a horrible guy - just… in his own zone.” Masson seemed to be keeping information to himself, and had very limited intelligence on the case. I then addressed Marie Cardona, one of Meursault’s former co-workers, now rumored to be involved with him. She was also invited to the beach house and I wanted to hear her take because she has known Meursualt the longest. She stated that Meursault was “a genuine person deep down and even though he doesn’t show much emotion,” he can’t be labeled as the killer that this case has made him out to be. She did however think it was “what he went through” made him unable “to convey [certain] emotions.” Meursault had lost his mother just weeks before, which made him more susceptible to feeling taken over by unreality and grief. I wanted to gain even more insight into the actual evidence of the case itself, so I reached out to Mersualt’s Head Defense Attorney. Isabel N. explained that “Meursault did not bring a gun, so any intention he had… wasn’t to kill anyone.” He wasn’t willful or premeditated in his actions which she also described was one of the accusations when being charged with first degree murder. Due to lawyer-client confidentiality she couldn’t disclose anymore information about the case.

“Meursault did not bring a gun, so any intention he had… wasn’t to kill anyone” - Isabel N.

I decided to get the opposition’s point of view on this case, so I connected with one of the victim’s friends an Arab involved in the case. He claims that he was “just looking after [the victim] hiding in the bush when” he saw “ Meursault come in and shoot.” The Head Prosecution Attorney, I spoke with after, had advised him not to divulge too much about the case so I left it at that. He seemed very nervous, confused, and agitated by the case, so I wondered if there was more to his side, and I guess we will find out in court. I got a little more intelligence on the case when I was able to address the Head Prosecution Attorney Caitlyn N. She conveyed that one of the witnesses, Marie Cordona, can testify to the fact that he is “ abusive… from how he doesn’t show emotion to her.” She also said that they will call up the victim's friend the Arab to prove that Meursault was the one to fire the shot. Lastly she most likely will call up Monsieur Raymond to speak about a recent fight that took place before the murder. She seemed to be very confident in her evidence and the trial proceedings.

Lastly I was able to briefly speak to Meursault himself wo believes that he himself will plead not guilty to the charge of first degree murder. He says that he “ was not in the right state of mind as [his] mom just passed away, and [he] was pretty depressed then.” In addition, he claims that he “wasn’t the one who initially brought the gun,” and had no intention to kill anyone. In the end the case will most likely bring a charge upon the accused, and pardon for some of the witnesses. Stay tuned for the case that is taking place Thursday November 20th to learn more.

MERSAULT'S CRIME

“But I fired four shots more into the inert body, on which they left no visible trace” (Camus 76). Meursault is curently being put on trial for first degree murder. First degree muder consists of three basic elements: willfulness, deliberation, and premeditation. Mersault’s crime consists of all three elements, making him guilty of the first degree murder of the Arab.

“But I fired four shots more into the inert body, on which they left no visible trace” - Mersault

Some may wonder, did Meursault purposefully murder the Arab? Yes. The first shot he fired instantly killed the Arab. However, he continued to fire four more shots into his body despite him being dead already. If he did not intentionally want to kill the Arab, he would not have continued shooting his dead body. Mersault’s malicious action to continue shooting the inert body conveys his “willfulness” and intentions on making sure the Arab was dead.

On another note, Meursault took his friend, Raymond’s, gun, put it in his pocket, and murdered the Arab. Meursault deliberately took Raymond’s gun and shot the Arab when Raymond specifically told Meursault not to shoot anybody, especially the Arabs. Raymond was aware that the Arabs were watching both Meursault and himself, however, he did not want to use any force or violence with them. The fact that Meursault still used Raymond’s gun to kill the Arab contradicts with what Raymond specifically told him not to do. This furthermore provides more evidence towards Meursault’s deliberate intent on murdering the Arab.

IT WAS DONE IN SELF DEFENSE

“Just one step forward. And then the Arab drew his knife and held it up toward me,” explained Meursault. Now, if anybody was in this same life threatening situation, their survival instincts would kick in immediately. The most prominent response comes from our nervous system: the fight or flight response. Everybody has one, and Meursault’s nervous system just happened to be programed to have him fight back to protect his life. It is because of this reason that he drew the revolver.

“Just one step forward. And then the Arab drew his knife and held it up toward me” - Mersault

Meursault is being put on trial for first-degree murder, defined as an intentional murder that is premeditated and carried out willfully and with malice. He is not guilty though. Did he plan ahead to kill the Arab? The answer is no. Some of you may be wondering why he had the revolver in his pocket then in the first place. Well, it doesn’t belong to him. He was carrying it for his acquaintance, Raymond, because he did not want Raymond to pull the gun on a group of people before they even made a move. The fact that Meursault did not want to witness someone being murdered in cold blood shows that he has a conscious and did not plan to pull the trigger on someone. Because his actions show that he obviously was not planning on using the gun, he cannot be found guilty of first degree murder since he does not fit the requirement of “premeditation”.

The way Meursault describes the incident, with his “grip closed on the revolver” as he got ready to make a decision that would determine the fate of his life, shows that he did it as a form of self-preservation. Furthermore, he could not even see the Arab because of the light reflecting off of the knife blinding him. Since he wasn’t able to see where he was shooting, we can reasonably infer that he pulled the trigger in hopes of sending a warning to the Arab not to come any closer with the knife. Therefore, him hitting the Arab with the first shot could have been an accident, disqualifying this from being a first-degree murder case.

Now, I am not denying that he is responsible for the death of the Arab, but I am saying that he is not guilty of first-degree murder because it is too high of a crime to charge him with. If he should be charged with anything, then it should be voluntary manslaughter. Voluntary manslaughter is defined as the killing of someone due to the heat of passion, under circumstances that emotional or mental disturbance. This crime fits the description of the sequence of events way better than first-degree murder because, as I’ve already proved, Meursault was not planning on killing anyone, but when the Arab pulled out his weapon and held it up, a surge of emotions must have rushed through his body because he felt like his life was being threatened and, therefore, acted in the heat of the moment to save his life.

TIES AND CAMUS

French-Arab relations have been very complicated in Algeria's history and the ripples from it are still felt today. It all kicked off in the 19th century with the French invasion. The French came and took Algiers in 1830, marking the beginning of a new era. There were efforts from the new colonizers to convert the Muslim population to christianity, which angered them. Their whole society was almost being reinvented, with many Europeans coming into Algeria. France went on to try and westernize Algerian society, shifting the family dynamic for many Muslim families. Women were empowered to do more than just be stay at home mom’s, which was expected of them before. The seizing of Algerian farmland and giving it to Europeans also angered many Muslims. All of this would later boil down to the Algerian war of independence.

The oppression many faced caused riots, and before France knew it they had a civil war on their hands.
